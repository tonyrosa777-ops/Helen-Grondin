## Section 1 — Executive Summary

Helen Grondin has a rare, time-sensitive opportunity to become the dominant health sharing advocate in New Hampshire — a state where **no local health sharing consultant currently ranks for any relevant search term**. The 2026 ACA subsidy expiration has triggered an average 21% premium increase for NH marketplace plans, pushing unsubsidized individual premiums above $491/month and family plans past $1,500/month. This creates an enormous wave of price-shocked self-employed workers, COBRA refugees, and families who are the exact audience for Impact Health Sharing's $73/month individual starting price. The competitive landscape is wide open: two existing Impact Health Sharing advocate websites are anonymous, template-built affiliate pages with no personal brand, no educational content, and design quality rated 3.5–4 out of 10.

The primary conversion goal — booking consultations — is strongly supported by the market reality that health sharing requires significant trust-building before enrollment. Consumers researching health sharing are terrified by high-profile fraud cases (Aliera/Trinity in NH itself, Liberty HealthShare's $140M scandal, Sharity's bankruptcy) and the fundamental fear that sharing is "voluntary" with no legal guarantee of payment. The winning strategy is to position Helen as a **trusted local guide** who addresses fears directly, leads with transparency rather than hype, and converts through education-first content paired with frictionless consultation booking. Every competitor organization uses impersonal enrollment funnels; Helen's personal, consultative approach is the differentiator.

Compliance is the highest-priority risk. New Hampshire lacks a dedicated health care sharing ministry safe harbor statute, meaning the NHID retains authority to classify any health sharing product as unauthorized insurance. The NHID has already shut down Aliera/Trinity and issued orders against Jericho Share. Helen's site must carry prominent "not insurance" disclaimers on every page, avoid all insurance terminology, and follow the disclosure models of Sedera and Solidarity HealthShare. The FTC holds individual marketers — not just organizations — liable for deceptive advertising, making proper disclosure not just a legal nicety but a personal liability shield.

### Buying Triggers
1. **COBRA sticker shock** — receiving the COBRA election notice with $1,200–$2,200/month pricing creates immediate, urgent search behavior
2. **ACA renewal premium jump** — the 2026 subsidy expiration caused after-subsidy costs to nearly double for many NH residents, from ~$150/month to ~$254/month average
3. **Leaving a corporate job for self-employment** — the single most common trigger across all forum research; loss of employer coverage forces an immediate healthcare decision
4. **Annual open enrollment frustration** — every November, families re-confront how broken the system feels, creating a seasonal conversion window
5. **Major life event outside open enrollment** — divorce, job loss, aging off parent's plan, or moving to NH creates urgency and year-round enrollment is a health sharing advantage
6. **Spouse's employer drops family coverage or increases employee contribution** — secondary breadwinner's employer cutting benefits shifts the household search
7. **Dave Ramsey or personal finance content exposure** — Ramsey Solutions' endorsement of health sharing creates a steady pipeline of people who've heard the concept and want local guidance

### Buying Blockers
1. **"What if they just don't pay?"** — the #1 fear across every forum, every review site; the voluntary nature of sharing with no legal guarantee of payment is the fundamental objection
2. **Aliera/Trinity scandal awareness (NH-specific)** — Keith Meehan of Rochester, NH was left with $230,000 in unpaid medical bills; this story was covered by NPR, NHPR, and the Union Leader and is locally known
3. **Pre-existing condition anxiety** — consumers with any health history fear being denied sharing for conditions classified as pre-existing, with 12–36 month waiting periods creating genuine risk
4. **"It's not real insurance" psychological barrier** — even people who intellectually understand the savings cannot emotionally accept leaving the insurance framework; the word "insurance" carries deep psychological safety
5. **Provider confusion and self-pay friction** — fear of having to explain health sharing to every doctor, being treated as uninsured, and personally managing bill negotiations
6. **Sent to collections while waiting for sharing** — multiple real stories of 60–180+ day processing times during which hospitals send members to collections, destroying credit
7. **Industry fraud precedent** — Liberty HealthShare's $140M+ in payments to family-owned vendors, Sharity's bankruptcy, Medical Cost Sharing Inc.'s FBI seizure collectively poison the well for all health sharing organizations

### Pricing Strategy Analysis

Impact's **$73/month starting price is the lowest branded entry point** among mainstream health sharing organizations (only Solidarity's catastrophic-only SHIELD at $67 is lower, but it covers nothing below the $12,000 threshold). This is Impact's most powerful marketing number. However, the $73 figure is only available to the youngest age bracket (18–29), and realistic pricing for Helen's primary persona (age 32–44 with family) will be **$378–$628+/month** depending on family size and PRA selection. The family price comparison is where Impact is less competitive — Zion at $295/month and Solidarity at $275/month undercut Impact's $378/month family starting price. Impact's 10% co-share after PRA (up to $5,000/year household cap) and the $500K annual sharing limit are also less generous than Zion's unlimited sharing and Knew Health's claimed no-maximum policy.

Competitors anchor pricing in three distinct ways. Liberty leads with **program variety** — six tiers create the impression of choice and customization, with Freedom at $89 serving as the low anchor. Knew Health leads with **stability** — "We haven't raised prices in 6+ years" turns price into a trust signal. Zion leads with **simplicity** — two membership types, three IUA options, interactive calculator. The most effective pattern across the market is leading with the lowest possible monthly number, then immediately showing annual savings versus the prospect's current plan.

### Recommended Pricing Strategy for the Website

Lead the homepage with **"Starting at $73/month"** as the anchor — this is Impact's strongest competitive number and should appear in the hero section. Immediately follow with a comparison frame: show a three-column visual comparing Impact ($73–$628/mo) against ACA unsubsidized ($330–$2,000+/mo) and COBRA ($584–$1,700/mo) using NH-specific numbers. On the dedicated pricing page, present a **savings calculator** where visitors enter their current monthly premium and family size to see estimated annual savings — this personalizes the value proposition. Always show annual savings totals, not just monthly: "$14,064 saved per year" is more emotionally impactful than "$1,172/month difference." Acknowledge the family pricing gap honestly — Impact's $378/month family price isn't the cheapest, but the no-network restriction, **70%+ bill repricing**, and 90/10 cost split create total-cost advantages that should be shown in worked examples. Never hide the PRA, co-share, or annual limit — transparency is what differentiates Helen from organizations that got sued for burying the fine print.

## COMPLIANCE APPENDIX — CRITICAL

### NH-Specific Legal Requirements

New Hampshire **does not have a dedicated health care sharing ministry (HCSM) safe harbor statute**. Unlike approximately 30 other states, NH has no "RSA 126-V" or similar chapter explicitly exempting HCSMs from insurance regulation. The NH Insurance Department (NHID) applies the **federal ACA criteria** (Section 5000A(d)(2)(B)) to determine whether an organization qualifies for exemption: it must be a 501(c)(3) nonprofit, members must share common ethical or religious beliefs, medical expenses must have been shared continuously since December 31, 1999, and the organization must provide a written disclaimer that it is not insurance.

The applicable NH statutes the NHID uses to regulate health sharing marketing include: **RSA 400-A:3** (general regulatory authority of Insurance Commissioner), **RSA 400-A:17 et seq.** (enforcement powers), **RSA 417:6 et seq.** (unfair trade practices in insurance — prohibits misrepresentations and deceptive advertising), **RSA 541-A:31** (administrative enforcement procedure), and **RSA 358-A** (NH Consumer Protection Act — gives the Attorney General power to bring action for deceptive trade practices). Because NH lacks a specific HCSM statute, the NHID retains full authority to classify any HCSM product as unauthorized insurance subject to full regulation.

The NHID Consumer Alert (January 2022) from Commissioner John Elias stated: *"I urge consumers to proceed with caution when purchasing health coverage options outside of Affordable Care Act compliant plans."* The alert warned that if an HCSM does not meet the federal exemption criteria (e.g., is for-profit, or doesn't date to pre-1999), it may be operating as an unlicensed insurance company. The NHID was pursuing regulatory action against HCSMs as a **priority for the 2024 legislative session**, signaling increasing scrutiny.

### FTC Requirements

The FTC applies **Section 5 of the FTC Act** (prohibiting unfair or deceptive acts or practices) and **Section 12** (false advertising) to all health-related marketing, including health sharing. Core requirements:

**Truthfulness:** All advertising must be truthful and not misleading. Both express and implied claims carry the same legal weight. If an ad has more than one reasonable interpretation, the advertiser must substantiate each one.

**Material omissions:** Deceiving a consumer by what you DON'T say is equally actionable as what you DO say. Omitting material information — such as the fact that sharing is voluntary and not guaranteed — is deceptive if it would change a consumer's decision.

**Clear and conspicuous standard (16 CFR § 255.5):** Any disclosures needed to prevent deception must be clear and conspicuous. Disclosures cannot be buried in fine print, hidden behind multiple clicks, in small font relative to main claims, or euphemistically worded. The FTC evaluates disclosures based on **consumer understanding**, not marketer's intent.

**Individual liability:** The FTC can hold liable not just the organization but **individual marketers, owners, corporate officers, distributors, and intermediaries** involved in deceptive marketing. This means Helen personally — not just Impact Health Sharing — could face FTC enforcement if the site's marketing is found to be deceptive.

Whether you receive any sharing for medical expenses and whether or not this program continues to operate, you are always personally responsible for the payment of your own medical bills. This is not a legally binding agreement to reimburse or indemnify you for medical expenses you incur.
